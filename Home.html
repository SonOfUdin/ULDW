Welcome to the webpage of the 2022 Urhobo Language Development workshop. ULDW is an annual workshop organised by the Urhobo Studies Association (USA), Delta State University, Abraka, through supports from spirited individuals and organisarions. The workshop aims to bring together Urhobo language teachers, enthusiasts and activists from across Delta Central and beyond to network and discuss on how to develop the Urhobo language for use in education (either as a medium of instruction or as a subject of study). Thus, the goals of the workshop includes:

  1. To promote discussions on revitalisation, growth and development of the Urhobo language, and language inclusion in all spheres of communication.
  2. To create a movement for cooperation with the aim of identifying strategies to accelerate the teaching and learning of Urhobo.
  3. To enhance Urhobo language teaching, learning, acquisition, and research.

This year's event (#ULDW22) which is both in-person and online seeks to address the twin issues of Urhobo writing system and scheme of work for teaching the Urhobo language in basic schools. Details about online participation can be found below.

SYNOPSIS OF PLENARIES

  1. Phonological issues in Urhobo Orthography Design
    Vowel elision, glide formation, the dual behavior of /e, o/, tone, and other phonological phenomena have implications for Urhobo orthography. One of the vowels in a sequence, especially the ﬁrst, gets deleted if it is not a high vowel (abo#emuo > abemuo ‘wrestling’); but becomes a glide if it is a high vowel (ikũ#esiri > ikwesiri 'goodnews'). ‘e’ & ‘o’ behave sometimes as non-high vowels (get deleted as in se#ọbe > sọbe ‘read book‘) or as high vowels (realized as glide as in re#ọne > rjọne ‘eat yam‘). Tone plays both lexical and functional roles in Urhobo, like distinguishing the meaning of words as in òdẹ̀ 'name' vs ódẹ̀ ‘yesterday’; or marking some grammatical categories such as tenseiaspect (mì sí wé ‘i pulled you‘ vs mǐ sí wé ‘i am pulling you‘). Phonetic vowel length (mǐ sí wé ‘i am pulling you‘) & contrastive nasal vowel also play a role in Urhobo. These phenomena can have an effect on ﬂuent reading, word identiﬁcation, and ambiguity; thus, this forum will address them, with special emphasis on the following: (a) should elided vowels be omitted? (this can improve ﬂuency but hinders word identiﬁcation & disambiguation as in de#ame ‘buy water‘ vs da#ame ‘drink water’ in dame; (b) should instance of ‘e/o’ as low vowels be represented differently from ‘e/o’ as high vowels?; (c) should tone be partlyiﬁllly marked or unmarked‘? if marked, how?; (d) should only basic tone be marked?; etc.

  2. Morphological/Syntactic issues in Urhobo Orthography Design
    This forum will discuss morphological issues which can have an impact on Urhobo orthography design, including word division, compounding, afﬁxation (in relation to ATR harmony) and the representation of grammatieal categories such as clitic, tense, aspect, etc. These phenomena can also impact on ﬂuent reading, word identiﬁcation, & ambiguity. Thus, these issues will be addressed, with particular focus on the following: (a) how should compound words be represented? hyphenated? (b) what word division principle should be adopted‘? (c) how should different grammatical categories be marked‘?, etc. There is also a pulse break in Urhobo utterances involving the use of ‘e’ such as Otiti chere emu vs Otiti chere emu. How should this be represented (with the pulse marker or word should be wholly represented)?

  3. How Should We Write Loanwords in Urhobo:
    Words are often modified when borrowed, especially when they are not "compatible" with the phonology of the borrowing language. According to Hosken (2003), one of the hardest issues an orthography has to deal with is how to spell words which are imported from another language. Do the words keep their original spelling or are they spelled according to how they sound when spoken in the language we are describing? This forum therefore seeks to not only answer this question but also proffer ideas on how to represent the different modifications, such as sound modification, vowel insertion, non-ﬁnal coda deletion (with particular focus on nasal coda), etc.

  4. Scheme of Work for Basic Education in Urhobo:
    A scheme of work's primary purpose is to assist teachers in proactively planning and sequencing their classes. By doing so, they can guarantee that the curriculum objectives are met and that all course material is taught before the school year closes. Thus, having developed a curriculum for the Urhobo language, it is also essential to chart a path on how to work out a scheme of work from the curriculum.

  5. Teaching Urhobo to Learners with English Background:
    Most students learning Urhobo already have some level of literacy in English. Thus, they come into the Urhobo learning environment with this background, which can be harnessed to strengthen their learning experiences. The discussion in this forum will thus revolve around how such a background can be optimally utilized in teaching the various topics and achieving learning objectives.

  6. Issues in Urhobo Language Testing and Assessment:
    Testing is a broad category of testing that assesses aspects of a person's ability to understand or communicate in a particular language. This forum will discuss how to best assess students taking Urhobo in basic schools, bearing in mind that the recent Delta State Basic Education Certificate Examination on the Urhobo language is characterized by criticisms, centred mainly on the content of the examination.
